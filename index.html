KZ2 Race Engineering — how to use this app. This is a complete race engineering tool covering session logging, AiM MyChron telemetry analysis, carburetor diagnosis, video review, and AI-powered setup analysis. Each section below explains what a tab does, how to use it step by step, and why the information matters. Tap any heading to expand it.
Log Session
Recording what you ran, in the conditions you ran it

What it does

The Log Session tab is your session notebook. Every time you go on track — test day, practice, qualifying, or race — you record the setup you ran, the conditions, and the outcome. Over time this builds a searchable database that lets you predict the right setup before you even leave the paddock.

Filling it in

Session information covers date, time, track, layout, driver, session type, and wet/dry conditions. Be consistent with track names — always "FEKC" rather than sometimes "Fulbeck" — because filtering relies on exact matches. The video filename field lets you link the session to a GoPro or camera clip so you can find the right footage later from the Video tab.

Tags are optional labels you add by typing a word and pressing Enter. Use them for anything that might be useful to filter on later — round-3, wet-tyres, new-engine, chassis-change. You can filter the database by tag to quickly pull all sessions of a particular type.

Weather fields (temperature, humidity, pressure) are the three numbers that define air density. Air density controls how much oxygen enters the engine per stroke, which directly determines how rich or lean your carburetor is running. Fill these in accurately — the Jetting Calculator depends on them entirely.

Engine — select the engine you ran from the dropdown. If your engine isn't listed, use the Manage Engines button to add it. The app tracks cumulative hours across sessions and alerts you as you approach rebuild intervals.

Carburetor fields — record exactly what you fitted: main jet, pilot jet, needle, clip position, emulsion tube, float weight, slide, choke jet, fuel type, and ethanol percentage. The Live Diagnosis panel updates in real time as you type, flagging any lean or incompatible combinations before you start the engine.

Results fields — best lap, peak EGT, mid EGT, peak RPM, and peak speed. If you import a telemetry CSV and link it to this session in the Telemetry tab first, these fields auto-fill from the data and are highlighted in teal to show they came from telemetry rather than manual entry.

Notes — free text for the driver and engineer separately. Write anything that doesn't fit elsewhere: "engine felt flat after lap 6", "try softer front axle next time", "kart understeering through hairpin". Notes from previous sessions at the same track become invaluable when you return.

💡 Best practice: Fill in weather and carb fields before going on track. The live diagnosis will catch anything obviously wrong — a dangerously lean main jet, an incompatible needle/tube combination — before the engine is running.

Live diagnosis

As you fill in the carburetor fields, the diagnosis panel below them updates instantly. Each item is colour-coded: red = danger (could damage the engine), amber = caution (worth investigating), green = correct, teal = informational. Checks include: main jet vs factory minimum, needle clip lean/rich assessment, emulsion tube compatibility with the needle, annulus cross-check (see Setup tab help), float weight, engine hours, air density vs jetting, EGT assessment, and fuel type. The panel is sorted with the most urgent issues at the top.

Saving the session

Click Save Session to store everything. Sessions are saved to your browser's local storage and persist between visits. Use the Database tab's JSON export to back up your data — browser storage can be cleared accidentally, so export after every event.

Database
Searching, comparing and backing up your session history

What it does

The Database tab shows every session you've logged in a searchable, sortable table. Filter by track, session type, driver, or tag; sort by fastest lap, date, or peak EGT. Click the eye icon on any row to open a full detail view — and edit any field if you need to correct something after the fact.

Filtering and sorting

Filter by track to see everything you've run at a particular circuit — essential preparation before a return visit. Sort by fastest lap to see immediately which session produced your best time and what you were running. Sort by peak EGT to quickly identify sessions where the engine was running hot, with the EGT column colour-coded red above your danger threshold and amber at warning level. The tag filter lets you group sessions by theme — all "round-3" entries, for example, or all "wet" sessions.

Engine management

The Manage Engines button (also accessible from the Log Session tab) opens the engine tracker. Add each engine with a name or serial number and set your rebuild intervals for top-end and bottom-end separately. The app accumulates hours from each logged session and alerts you as you approach the intervals. This is important for carburetor calibration: worn rings reduce compression, which increases lean risk — the diagnosis engine accounts for this when engine hours are high.

Performance trends

Once you have three or more sessions logged, trend charts appear below the table showing lap time progression and peak EGT history across sessions. A downward trend in lap times alongside stable EGT confirms setup improvements are working. A rising EGT trend across sessions with no setup change may indicate engine wear.

Cross-session telemetry comparison

If you've imported and linked telemetry CSVs (see Telemetry), a comparison panel appears at the bottom of the Database tab. Select two or three sessions to overlay their speed, RPM, and EGT traces on the same chart. This is the most direct way to see the effect of a jetting change — you can visually compare exactly where on the lap an EGT spike appeared before and after a main jet adjustment.

Export and import

Use Export JSON to download your entire session database as a single file. Save this to cloud storage after every event — it is your only backup, and browser storage can be wiped by clearing your browser data. Use Import JSON on another device to load those sessions. Duplicate sessions are automatically skipped, so you can safely merge databases.

Telemetry
Importing and analysing your AiM MyChron data

Exporting a CSV from Race Studio 3

  1. Connect your MyChron5S 2T to Race Studio 3 via USB or Wi-Fi and download the session.
  2. In RS3, go to Preferences → Data Download → Advanced and enable CSV export.
  3. Select the session in the database, right-click and choose Export → CSV. Choose RS2Analysis Style even if you're using RS3.
  4. Ensure column headers are in English (check your RS3 language setting if needed).
  5. Drag and drop the .csv file onto the upload zone in the Telemetry tab.
💡 The app automatically detects these channels if present: Time, RPM, Speed, EGT (exhaust temperature), GPS Latitude, GPS Longitude, Lateral G, Longitudinal G. No configuration needed — just drop the file.

Linking to a session

Before or after importing, use the Link to session dropdown to match the telemetry to a logged session. Once linked, best lap time, peak EGT, peak RPM, and peak speed automatically populate that session record, and the telemetry is available for cross-session comparison in the Database tab.

Lap selection

The app automatically identifies flying laps (excluding out-laps and in-laps). Use the lap selector buttons at the top to choose which laps to display. The fastest lap is pre-selected as the reference. You can overlay up to ten laps simultaneously. Laps from comparison sessions appear in a different colour — gold for the primary session, teal for the first comparison, green for the second.

Time vs Distance axis

Toggle between Time and Distance on the x-axis. Distance mode aligns corners at the same track position regardless of pace — two laps will show the same corner at the same x-position, making it easier to see where one lap gains on another. Time mode is better for absolute timing analysis and EGT review.

What each chart tells you

ChartWhat to look for
Speed traceThe overall shape of the lap. Compare minimum corner speeds — higher minimum speed means better corner entry and faster exit. Consistent traces across laps mean consistent driving.
Lap deltaWhere one lap is faster or slower than the reference. A line rising above zero means time is being lost there. This is your primary tool for identifying which corners to focus on.
RPM traceKZ2 should spend most of the lap above 13,000 RPM. If RPM flattens or drops approaching the rev limit, the engine may be lean (EGT will confirm) or gearing is too tall. RPM dropping mid-corner after the pipe hits can indicate rich needle zone.
EGT traceYour primary engine health tool. Target range is 480–580°C. The trace shows exactly when during the lap EGT rises — spikes on long straights at WOT point to main jet; elevated EGT through corners at partial throttle points to needle and clip position.
EGT vs RPM scatterPlots EGT against RPM for the fastest lap. A healthy engine shows a tight cloud in the blue/low-amber zone. Points scattered into the red at high RPM confirm a lean main jet.
Longitudinal GPositive = accelerating, negative = braking. Shows how hard and how late the driver is braking into corners. Requires the 6-axis IMU in the MyChron5S 2T.

Consistency analysis

The consistency panel shows the standard deviation of lap times (lower is better), the percentage of laps within 0.3 seconds of the best, and a trend indicating whether you were getting faster or slower through the session. Rising lap times typically indicate tyre degradation or driver fatigue. Falling times indicate the driver was still finding the limit — there may be more to come.

Corner analysis

The app automatically detects corners from the speed trace and shows the minimum speed at each corner, with deltas between laps colour-coded green (faster) and red (slower). This tells you precisely which corners gained or lost time, rather than just seeing the overall lap delta.

Ideal lap

The theoretical best lap is assembled from the fastest sector of every lap in the session. The gap between your current best and the ideal lap shows how much time is available if you strung together your best in each part of the track. A large gap suggests consistency issues; a small gap suggests you're near the limit with the current setup.

⚠️ If you see EGT readings above 620°C in the trace, do not run further laps without increasing the main jet.
Heat Map
GPS-based track map with channel overlays

What it does

The Heat Map tab draws a map of the circuit from your MyChron's GPS data and colour-codes every point on the track by a data channel of your choice. This makes it immediately obvious which parts of the track are fast, where the engine is under strain, and where time is being lost — information that is easy to miss when looking at traces alone.

Requirements

You need a CSV that includes GPS Latitude and GPS Longitude columns. The MyChron5S 2T records GPS at 25Hz, which is more than adequate for track mapping. Ensure GPS has a clear sky view when you start the session — poor satellite lock at the beginning can distort the first lap or two.

Hovering and cross-referencing

Move your cursor over the heat map to see the channel value at that exact track position. When you hover over the telemetry charts in the Telemetry tab at the same time, a white dot on the heat map shows your corresponding track position — useful for understanding which corner a spike in the data corresponds to.

Cross-session comparison

Switch to Compare mode to overlay GPS traces from multiple sessions. Each session gets its own colour. Diverging lines at a corner mean different racing lines are being taken — a sign that chassis balance may have changed between sessions. A sector comparison table beneath the map shows each session's channel values across 10 equal track sectors.

Video
Reviewing on-board footage with synchronised telemetry overlay

What it does

The Video tab plays on-board footage with a live telemetry dashboard overlaid — speed, RPM, EGT, gear, and lateral G all update frame by frame as the video plays. It also shows a live track map dot moving around the circuit. This links what you see on screen directly to what the data is showing — far more revealing than looking at traces and video separately.

Loading a video

Click Choose video and select the footage file from your device. The video stays local — it is never uploaded anywhere. Supported formats are MP4, MOV, and AVI (browser dependent; MP4 is most reliable). If you noted the video filename in the Log Session tab, it appears here as a reminder so you can find the right clip quickly.

Synchronising to telemetry

For the dashboard overlay and track map to work, you need telemetry imported in the Telemetry tab. Once telemetry is loaded, a Sync control appears. Use it to align the video to the telemetry — pause the video at a known moment (crossing the start line, for example) and adjust the offset until the telemetry dashboard matches. Once set, synchronisation holds for the entire session.

Lap selection

Once synchronised, use the lap pill buttons below the video to jump directly to the start of any flying lap. The app calculates the video timestamp for each lap start from the telemetry timing data.

Playback controls

Speed buttons let you play at 0.5×, 1×, 1.5×, or 2× speed. Slower speeds are useful for analysing braking points and corner entry in detail. Frame-by-frame stepping (using the keyboard arrow keys when the video is focused) allows you to examine a specific moment precisely.

Dual video comparison

Click Add comparison video to load a second video alongside the first — typically a different lap or a different driver in the same kart. Both videos play synchronised side by side. Each has its own telemetry dashboard and track map dot, displayed in different colours (gold for session 1, teal for session 2). A delta panel between them shows the real-time time gap as both videos play.

The Full-screen compare button opens both videos in a fullscreen split-screen mode with the telemetry overlay centred between them — the clearest way to do a direct side-by-side driving comparison.

💡 The most useful thing to look for in dual video comparison: whether corner entry speed, braking point, and throttle application point are the same between the two laps. If the lines are identical but one lap is faster, the difference is usually throttle application confidence — which is a chassis balance question, not a driver error.
Setup
Standalone carb diagnosis and jetting calculator

What it does

The Setup tab contains two tools: a standalone carburetor diagnosis panel (the same engine that powers the live diagnosis in Log Session, but with more room to experiment) and a jetting calculator that predicts what to change when conditions differ from a known-good baseline.

How a VHSH30 works — the four circuits

The Dellorto VHSH30 does not have a single fuel setting. It has several overlapping circuits, each controlling fuel delivery at a different throttle position. A change to the main jet only affects full throttle. A change to the needle clip only affects mid-throttle. Diagnosing a problem correctly requires knowing which circuit is responsible for that throttle range.

Throttle positionPrimary controlHow to adjust
0–10% (idle)Pilot jet + idle emulsion tubeChange pilot jet size
10–25% (off-idle transition)Slide cutawayChange slide spec
25–50% (needle base)Needle straight diameter (A dimension)Change needle type
50–75% (needle taper)Needle taper profile + clip positionMove clip down (richer) or up (leaner)
40–90% (overlap)Emulsion tube (DP vs DQ, tube ID)Change emulsion tube spec
75–100% (WOT)Main jetIncrease size (richer) or decrease (leaner)

Lean vs rich

Lean means not enough fuel relative to air. The engine runs hot (high EGT), feels snappy, but is at risk of piston seizure. Rich means too much fuel — the engine four-strokes (burbles at full throttle), feels sluggish, and produces black smoke. Rich is usually safe but slow. Correct means EGT in the 480–590°C range and the engine pulling cleanly to the rev limit.

Clip position

The needle has 5 clip grooves. Clip in groove 1 (top) = needle at its lowest = leanest mid-range. Clip in groove 5 (bottom) = needle at its highest = richest mid-range. Factory baseline for KZ2 is clip 3 (centre). Moving the clip to a lower number raises the needle and richens the mixture. Moving to a higher number lowers the needle and leans it.

Half-step adjustment: A small washer is fitted either above or below the clip. When the washer is above the clip it has no effect on needle height. When moved below the clip it raises the needle by half a groove — equivalent to half a clip position richer. This is logged in the app as position 2.5, 3.5, etc.

💡 Common confusion: "moving the clip down" means moving it to a lower groove number, which actually raises the needle — making the mixture richer. Think of it as the clip falling down the needle toward the tip, which lifts the needle body upward.

Emulsion tube (DP vs DQ)

The emulsion tube (also called the needle well or atomiser) controls how air and fuel are blended in the main circuit, particularly in the 40–90% throttle overlap zone. The factory KZ2 spec is DP268. The number is the internal diameter in hundredths of a millimetre — DP268 = 2.68mm bore. A DQ tube is a different design with a longer nozzle section that protrudes further into the venturi, delivering fuel later during throttle opening — making it leaner than a DP tube of the same bore number by the equivalent of approximately 6 main jet sizes at full throttle. Common race combinations pair a DQ tube with a richer needle (K28, K23) to compensate.

Annulus check — Dellorto's own rule

Dellorto specify that the main jet flow area must not fall below the annulus created by the needle tip inside the needle jet. The annulus is the ring-shaped gap between the needle jet bore and the needle tip — this is the fuel passage for the main circuit during partial throttle. If the main jet area is smaller than the annulus, when the throttle is opened sharply the main circuit cannot supply fuel fast enough, creating a momentary lean peak during the progression-to-high-speed transition (typically on corner exit at ¼–¾ throttle). This can cause hesitation, flat spots, or — in extremis — a lean seizure during acceleration.

For the standard KZ combination (DP268 needle jet, K98 needle): the annulus is calculated using the needle's straight section (A-dimension = 2.52mm), not the tip. Annulus = π/4 × (2.68² − 2.52²) = 0.655 sq mm. MJ180 area = 2.545 sq mm — well above the threshold. In practice all standard KZ main jets pass this check comfortably. The check becomes relevant if an unusually narrow needle jet bore is combined with a very wide needle straight section, leaving almost no clearance. The Live Diagnosis panel calculates this automatically for whatever combination you enter.

Float weight

The 9g float is the correct KZ standard — not a deviation. A heavier float sits lower in the fuel bowl, raising the fuel level, which enriches the mixture globally across all throttle positions. A lighter float lowers the fuel level and leans the mixture globally. Unless you are deliberately compensating for something else, stay at 9g.

Jetting calculator

The jetting calculator predicts what to change when conditions differ from a known-good baseline. Enter the baseline setup (main jet, clip position, conditions where it ran correctly) and the target conditions (today's temperature, humidity, pressure, altitude). The calculator shows the air density change between the two and recommends the adjusted main jet, clip position, emulsion tube, and pilot jet.

The underlying principle: denser air (cold, dry, low altitude) contains more oxygen per stroke, which leans the mixture — you need a larger main jet. Thinner air (hot, humid, high altitude) contains less oxygen — you need a smaller jet. Standard air density is 1.204 kg/m³ at 15°C, 0% humidity, 1013 mbar, sea level. The calculator shows your baseline and target as a percentage of this standard.

⚠️ The jetting calculator gives a starting point, not a definitive answer. Weather station data and the carburettor's actual response can vary. Always cross-check EGT after the first lap of any session with a new setup.
AI Analysis
Using Claude to analyse your session history

What it does

The AI Analysis tab connects to Anthropic's Claude AI and sends it your full session database — all logged setups, conditions, EGT readings, and lap times. Claude acts as an experienced KZ2 race engineer, reviewing your data, identifying patterns across sessions, and giving specific setup recommendations. It understands the TM R2 engine, the VHSH30 carburettor, the four-circuit throttle model, and the annulus rule — the same knowledge base that powers the diagnosis engine in this app.

Setting up your API key

  1. Go to console.anthropic.com and create an account.
  2. Navigate to API Keys and create a new key.
  3. Copy the key (it starts with sk-ant-api03-...) and paste it into the API key field at the top of the AI Analysis tab.
  4. Click Save. The key is stored only in your browser and sent only directly to Anthropic's API — never to any other server.
💡 Cost: Anthropic charges per API use. Each analysis request costs approximately $0.01 USD or less. There is no subscription — you only pay for what you use.

Analysis modes

Analyse all sessions — a comprehensive review of your full history: EGT patterns across conditions, which setups worked and which didn't, and a specific recommendation for your next session with exact jet sizes and clip positions.

Latest session — focused diagnosis of the most recent session only. Good for a quick post-session debrief when you want to know what to change before tomorrow.

Carb recommendation — asks Claude specifically for a jetting recommendation based on your EGT history and conditions. Covers main jet, clip position, emulsion tube, and pilot jet — not just the main jet in isolation.

EGT Alarm Thresholds
Setting your engine safety limits

What they do

The EGT thresholds (configured in the Settings gear icon at the top right) set three temperature limits used throughout the app — in chart reference lines, the session diagnosis, the database EGT colour coding, and the live carb diagnosis in Log Session. Changing them takes effect everywhere immediately and is saved to the browser.

ThresholdDefaultWhat it means
Danger620°CStop immediately. Above this risks piston seizure within seconds. Add two main jet sizes before the next run.
Warning590°CMonitor closely. The engine is running warm. Consider +2 main jet. Do not run longer stints or reduce your gearing (which increases engine load) until EGT is back in range.
Ideal minimum480°CBelow this the engine may be running rich. Symptoms: four-stroking (burbling) at full throttle, sluggish acceleration. If EGT is consistently below this and the engine is burbling, consider −2 main jet.
⚠️ These are post-session software thresholds. The app cannot alert you in real time during a session. Set live alarms on your MyChron5S 2T in Race Studio 3 at the same temperatures, so the alarm LED triggers on track if EGT exceeds the danger threshold.

Race weekend workflow

The evening before: Check the weather forecast. Open the Jetting Calculator, enter your most recent session at this track as the baseline, enter tomorrow's forecast conditions as the target, and note the recommended adjustments.

On arrival: Record actual ambient conditions. Update the jetting calculator with the real numbers. Fill in the Log Session form with today's intended setup — the live diagnosis will flag any concerns immediately.

Before each session: Confirm the carb setup matches what's in the Log Session form. Check the diagnosis panel is showing all-green before the engine starts.

After each session: Export the CSV from Race Studio 3 and import it in the Telemetry tab. Link it to the session — best lap, peak EGT, RPM, and speed will auto-populate. Review the EGT trace for spikes. If peak EGT is above 590°C, investigate before the next session.

Before qualifying: Use the Consistency panel to check whether the driver was still improving through practice or had plateaued — this tells you how much time might still be available with the same setup.

After the event: Export your session database to JSON via the Database tab and save it to cloud storage. This is your only backup.

Fuel guide — UK KZ2

The two fuels you are likely to encounter in UK KZ2 club racing:

FuelRONSGOxygenEthanolJetting vs RON99 E5Notes
RON99 E5 (Shell V-Power, Tesco Momentum)99~0.735~1.75%5%Reference — no changeUK standard KZ2 reference fuel. Factory baselines calibrated for this.

E10 pump (RON95)95~0.730~3.5%10%+2 main jet richerNot recommended for KZ2 competition. Leaner than E5, can destabilise oil suspension at high RPM.

The quantified rule of thumb (Sunoco Tech Corner): for every 2-point increase in SG, reduce fuelling by ~1%; for every 1% increase in oxygen content, increase fuelling by ~1%. These two effects act in opposite directions when comparing CFR to RON99 E5, which is why they cancel out.

For a fixed-timing KZ2 engine (homologation requires analogue ignition — no variable timing permitted) the octane difference between RON99 E5 and CFR provides no power advantage. You cannot advance timing to exploit the extra octane headroom. The value of CFR is protection against detonation and consistent jetting across a season.

Fuel additives — competition ban and why they don't work anyway

Motorsport UK and CIK regulations are unambiguous: the use of power boosting or octane boosting additives is expressly prohibited in competition. The only permitted addition to fuel for a two-stroke KZ2 engine is 2-stroke premix lubricant. Fuel testing can occur post-race; a positive result means disqualification and a minimum £500 testing cost charge to the driver.

For practice use the technical case against additives is equally clear. Toluene-based boosters have high solvent properties that attack reeds and crank seals in two-stroke engines. MMT-based boosters increase engine deposits and plug fouling risk. Neither type meaningfully raises MON (the relevant octane measure at high load and high RPM) — they primarily raise RON. Beyond these issues, the TM R2 runs fixed ignition timing by regulation, so even if an additive genuinely increased knock resistance there would be no mechanism to exploit it. Race fuel (CFR) is always a better solution if higher octane protection is genuinely needed.

Oil type and ratio — TM R2

The TM R2 specifies 4–5% synthetic oil, equivalent to 20:1 to 25:1. The standard running ratio is 25:1 (4%). Use 20:1 (5%) for new or recently rebuilt engines, or for high-stress events in very hot conditions.

Oil typeExamplesNotes
100% synthetic esterElf HTX 976+, Motul 800 2TPreferred by professional KZ teams. Burns cleanest, highest power output in dyno testing. HTX 976+ showed +1.5hp vs Castrol K997 in UK Superkart testing. 100% synthetic — matches TM R2 "synthetic" specification exactly.

💡 Does fuel type change the required oil ratio? No. Oil ratio is determined by the engine's lubrication requirements, not the fuel's chemistry. Switching between RON99 E5, Sunoco CFR, or E10 does not require an oil ratio adjustment. The 5% ethanol in E5 has no meaningful effect on oil suspension stability or lubrication at the concentrations used in KZ2.
💡 Does changing oil brand affect jetting? Negligibly. Within the same ratio (e.g. always 25:1), switching between synthetic oil brands has no meaningful effect on the air/fuel ratio. A significant ratio change (e.g. 20:1 vs 25:1) adds a small amount of combustible material to the charge, but this is absorbed by normal jetting variation and not worth compensating for.
